Smart move: Better to overpay than invest

Because you are naturally on track to pay off your loan completely, making voluntary overpayments is generally better than investing. It guarantees you avoid high compounding interest charges.

The "snowball" effect

If you can afford it, overpaying by £100 to £250 extra per month can shave years off your term and potentially save you thousands in interest.

You should avoid investing this extra overpayment cash for now, as the student loan interest rate is currently higher than most safe investment returns. Ensure you have a standard emergency cash fund built up first!

It's a contribution, not a credit card

The way student loans operate in the UK means they behave more like a temporary graduate tax than a traditional loan. Let's look at the facts directly from GOV.UK.

Financial anxiety is real

Seeing a large number attached to your name can trigger a stress response. But remember: this number cannot hurt you. It cannot take your house, it cannot ruin your credit, and it is safely wiped at the end of its term or if your circumstances permanently change.

Calm your thoughts down

1. Catch the thought: What is your brain telling you right now?
("I'll be paying this forever. I'm ruined. It's growing too fast.")

2. Challenge the evidence: Are your thoughts technically true based on the UK system?
("No, it gets completely wiped in {{ remainingYears }} years regardless of the balance. And if I lose my job tomorrow, my payments legally drop to £0 instantly.")

3. Reframe it:
"I don't have an impending crisis. I simply have a conditional 9% contribution on my higher earnings for a set period, in exchange for the degree that unlocked my career path."

Identify the mental traps

Anxiety often relies on "cognitive distortions"—mental filters that warp reality. Which of these might be tricking your mind right now?

Catastrophising: Assuming the absolute worst will happen.

Example: Believing this debt will completely ruin your financial future.

Fortune telling: Trying to predict the future negatively.

Example: Predicting you will never get a mortgage.

Mental filtering: Only paying attention to the scary parts.

Example: Focusing exclusively on the terrifying total balance while ignoring the wipe date.

Emotional reasoning: Believing that because you feel a certain way, it must be true.

Example: Feeling like you are drowning, so assuming the debt is actively dangerous.

Other: Something else?

The first step is simply understanding how your mind might be tricking you. By recognising these mental patterns, you take back control.

Challenge the thought

Let's look at the evidence. Read the facts below, then write down how they challenge your negative thought.

Structural reality check

  • It is a tax: It functions as a 9% graduate tax over a specific earning threshold, not a commercial bank loan.
  • The balance is a ghost: The debt is legally wiped after 30 or 40 years, regardless of how much you owe. The total figure rarely matters.
  • Credit safe: It does NOT go on your credit file and does NOT stop you from getting a mortgage (lenders just see it as a normal monthly tax deduction).
  • The safety net: If you lose your job or drop below the threshold, payments legally stop immediately. £0 income = £0 payments.

The reframe

Based on the evidence, write a new, balanced thought. It doesn't have to be overly positive, just realistic.

Example: "The total balance is high, but it functions like a temporary tax. It will not stop me from buying a house, and it will eventually be wiped."
